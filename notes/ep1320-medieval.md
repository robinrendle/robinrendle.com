Last week I picked up the [EP-1320 Medieval](https://teenage.engineering/products/ep-1320) and I’ve been tinkering with it all weekend. Besides it’s buck-wild look, it comes with a bunch of expertly recorded medieval samples: flutes, bowed harps, gitterns, reeds, trumpets, bells, battle toms, rowdy peasants, and coconut horse hooves. This makes it sound like a joke, a musical toy that immediately finds itself in the back of your closet, but what I love about this musical machine is how _seriously_ it takes itself. Yes, this is a medieval sampler! Get with the times!

The EP-1320 reminds me of programming, or when I learned how to `cd`, `ls`, `rm` in the command line. You figure out a few helpful words of this scary, foreign language and it opens up a world of possibilities. A new way of thinking quickly reveals itself. 

(You program the machine, and the machine programs _you_.)

So the EP-1320 is not simple. You either learn it’s way of doing things or go to hell. Like computers from before my time, this thing is modes-galore. Much to the horror of [Larry Tesler](https://interactions.acm.org/archive/view/july-august-2012/a-personal-history-of-modeless-text-editing-and-cut-copy-paste), you’re always in a mode and constantly switching between them: _sonus, manus, tempus, codex, claves, pocus_ (to make matters worse/better, every command and mode is labelled in Latin). There are songs, patterns, groups, and scenes: you must think in a very specific way about music to use the EP-1320. You have to read [the guide](https://teenage.engineering/guides/ep-1320). Study [the icons](https://teenage.engineering/guides/ep-1320/screen#3.1-icon-map). Program [the samples](https://teenage.engineering/apps/ep-sample-tool). 

And so, like programming, the EP-1320’s *inflexibility*, it’s almost complete and absolute stubbornness, is what makes it so charming.

Plus, ya know, the coconut horse hooves.
